Australia’s Changing Face

Australia is a nation shaped by migration, language, and growth. Over the past two decades, the population has surged not just in size but also in the rich mix of cultures and languages that call it home. From the bustling, multilingual cities of New South Wales and Victoria to the quieter communities of Tasmania and the Northern Territory, every state tells a different story.

Australians Speaking a Language Other than English at Home

The map illustrates the percentage of Australians who speak a language other than English at home, shaded using an orange gradient where darker tones represent higher proportions of non-English speakers. The Northern Territory records the highest proportion, reflecting the region’s strong Indigenous language presence alongside smaller migrant communities. Victoria follows closely, driven by Melbourne’s diverse migrant population while New South Wales also exhibits high linguistic diversity centred around Sydney. Western Australia, South Australia, Queensland and the Australian Capital Territory show moderate diversity, whereas Tasmania has the lowest proportions of non-English speakers. Overall, the distribution underscores how urbanisation, Indigenous heritageand international migration together shape Australia’s evolving cultural and language landscape.

Languages Spoken at Home and Overseas-Born Populations by State

The chart shows that Mandarin is by far the most widely spoken non-English language at home in Australia with nearly twice as many speakers as Arabic, the second most common language. Other prominent languages include Cantonese and Vietnamese, both reflecting Australia’s strong Asian migration links. At the lower end, Italian and Greek remain widely spoken among older migrant communities, differing by only about a thousand speakers. In total, over 7% of Australians speak one of these seven languages which highlights Australia as one of the world’s most linguistically diverse and multicultural societies.

The chart highlights distinct migration patterns across Australian states. New South Wales records the largest overseas born population overall with particularly high numbers of residents from Chinese Asia and New Zealand. The concentration of New Zealand born residents in New South Wales reflects its strong economic links, proximity across the Tasman and Sydney’s role as a major employment and migration hub. In comparison, Victoria shows a stronger representation from Southern Asia, mirroring Melbourne’s diverse and rapidly expanding South Asian community. At the other end of the spectrum, Tasmania, Northern Territory, and the Australian Capital Territory have smaller overseas-born populations though they still exhibit the same regional origins on a more modest scale.

Religious Affiliation in Australia

The treemap visualises the religious affiliations of Australians. Christianity remains the largest religious category with Catholicism forming the biggest segment within it. This reflects Australia’s historical ties to European settlement and Christian traditions that continue to influence cultural and community life today. However, the ‘No Religion / Secular’ category now occupies almost as much space as Christianity overall which is a striking sign of Australia’s growing secularisation. Younger generations increasingly identify as having no religious affiliation due to being influenced by social liberalism, education and cultural diversity.

Non-Christian faiths such as Islam, Hinduism and Buddhism also make up visible portions of the treemap, largely reflecting recent migration patterns from Asia and the Middle East which adds to Australia’s evolving religious landscape.

Population Growth & Migration Trends in Australia

Population Growth (2001–2024)

The chart highlights how Australia’s population has grown steadily since 2001. However, following the 2020 COVID-19 border closures, a clear disruption appears in 2021 as both New South Wales and Victoria recorded a temporary population decline unlike all other states which continued to grow.

This drop reflects in the halt of overseas migration which is a key driver of population growth in Sydney and Melbourne as well as domestic relocation as residents moved from large metropolitan areas to smaller states seeking affordability, lifestyle changes or to return home during the pandemic. Once restrictions eased, growth resumed which shows the strong role that migrant inflows and inter-state mobility play in shaping Australia’s demographic landscape.

Age Distribution of Australia’s Population

The chart shows the age distribution of Australia’s population, revealing a relatively young and balanced demographic structure. The largest age group is 30–34 years, reflecting Australia’s strong working age population driven by migration and stable birth rates in the early 1990s. Population numbers gradually decline with increasing age, with noticeable drops from the 70–74 years range onward. The smallest group is those aged 85 years and over which highlights the natural effects of ageing and mortality despite an overall increasing life expectancy. The pattern emphasises Australia’s continued reliance on younger adults and migrants to sustain its workforce and support an ageing population.
